SurgSTU

An Approach to Enriching Surgical Video Datasets for Fine-Grained Spatial-Temporal Understanding of Vision-Language Models

Lennart Maack*, 1 Alexander Schlaefer1
1 Institute of Medical Technology and Intelligent Systems, Hamburg University of Technology, Germany
*Corresponding author (lennart.maack@tuhh.de)
DataCV @ CVPR 2026 Workshop
Datasets Code Paper arXiv

Abstract

We introduce SurgSTU, a deterministic, template-driven pipeline that turns publicly available surgical-video annotations (CholecT50 + ProstaTD) into a large, fine-grained spatial-temporal question-answering dataset. The pipeline first normalizes every per-frame annotation into an Event Tuple — timestamp in seconds, instrument class, persistent instance id, normalized bounding box, verb, and target — then fills predefined task templates against continuity- and interestingness-filtered windows. A geometric instance-id tracker preserves same-class duplicates end-to-end; dataset-level rejection sampling damps modal-answer shortcuts on 14 categorical templates; explicit refusal samples discourage hallucination on absent instruments.

Applied to ~6,700 clips across laparoscopic cholecystectomy and robot-assisted prostatectomy, SurgSTU yields 179,912 QA conversations spanning five task families: spatial-temporal grounding, referred instrument-interaction captioning, spatial-temporal relation comparison, multi-choice (classes / existence / counting), and chain-of-thought interaction. Splits are leakage-safe at the procedure level. The pipeline is fully reproducible from a single random seed and does not depend on a language-model rewriter in any stage. We release the pipeline, the generated dataset, and a Qwen3-VL fine-tuning recipe as a downstream benchmark.

Pipeline at a glance

Stage 1

Event Tuples

Per-frame JSON annotations are normalized into (t_sec, c_i, inst_id, x1, x2, y1, y2, c_v, c_t) rows. A geometric instance-id tracker handles same-class duplicates.

Stage 2

Template-driven QA

Three deterministic generators (SpatioTemporal, MultiChoice, Interaction) fill QA templates against continuity-filtered windows. No LLM in the loop.

Stage 3

Bbox formatting

Emitted bboxes are converted to Qwen-style {"bbox_2d": [x1, y1, x2, y2]} in normalized [0, 1000] space.

Stage 4

Image-frame QA

A complementary single-frame stream (Caption / EasyDescription / SpatialAction) is sampled at a configurable FPS.

Stage 5

Leakage-safe splits

A single procedure-to-split map is applied to both modalities so no procedure leaks between train and test.

Design choices — FAQ

Reviewer-style questions about data quality, residual skews, and reproducibility. We surface honest numbers rather than over-prune the corpus.

The pipeline is fully template-driven — no LLM augmentation?

Correct. Every QA is produced by a small set of deterministic Python templates that read normalized event tuples and fill them in. No LLM rewrites or paraphrases the questions. This makes the dataset reproducible from a single seed (default 42) and decouples its quality from any third-party model.

How do you defend against the model learning the modal answer instead of looking at the video?

Phase 2.5 introduced per-procedure, per-correct-answer rejection sampling for 14 categorical templates (MOST_ACTIVE_ACCEPT, IDLE_DURATION_ACCEPT, etc.). The orchestrator probabilistically drops samples whose correct answer is over-represented for that procedure. Phase 2.6 re-tuned these maps after gates were tightened. Top-1 share dropped substantially — e.g. reverse_target 91 % → 51 %, idle_duration 93 % → 49 %.

Are any templates still skewed after rejection sampling? Be specific.

Yes — we surface them openly rather than over-prune the corpus. After Phase 2.6 iteration 2:

  • most_active_instrument (cholec) sits at 41 % on grasper
  • idle_duration (cholec) sits at 45 % on the [50%+] bucket
  • comparison (cholec) sits at 52 % on Yes
  • rel_change (cholec) sits at 46 % on maintained

Pushing them further would either cost additional dataset shrinkage (the Phase 2.6 retune already cost 11.3 %) or require new accept maps for templates not yet covered. We chose visibility over aggressive pruning.

Where do the bounding boxes come from? Are CholecT50 and ProstaTD annotated the same way?

No — and this matters. CholecT50 bboxes are pulled from CholecTrack20, an open companion release. ProstaTD bboxes are internally annotated pending an official bbox release for ProstaTD. The pipeline normalizes both to the same (x1, x2, y1, y2) order against the actual video stream's dimensions, but downstream consumers should be aware of the provenance asymmetry.

Why are the continuity gates so tight (0.6 normalized units/sec, 0.7 s gap)?

Phase 2.6 tightened the spatial-continuity gate from 1.0 to 0.6 normalized-diagonal/sec and the temporal-gap gate from 1.0 s to 0.7 s. The earlier values let bbox-jitter tracks through whose centroid moved unrealistically between frames. Tightening cost about 11.3 % of the corpus but produced cleaner trajectories — the rel_change template required the tightening before the "approximately maintained" class became meaningful (the REL_CHANGE_MIN_DELTA threshold also went 0.05 → 0.20).

There are ~5 % refusal samples in the dataset — why include them, and why that fraction?

Two refusal templates are wired in: refusal_absent_instrument (asked about an instrument that never appears in the clip) and refusal_no_specific_action (asked about a verb when the instrument is idling). Without them, a model learns to always produce a confident bounding box or verb. The 5 % target is a heuristic cap; higher rates push models toward over-refusal on legitimate queries.

Why was the velocity-estimation subcategory removed?

Bbox-centroid velocity in raw camera coordinates cannot separate genuine instrument motion from camera ego-motion — pan, zoom, and rotation are common in surgical video and bias the centroid. Rather than ship a metric the model could be right or wrong for the wrong reason, we removed gen_velocity entirely. The trajectory subcategory remains because it grounds in absolute extreme positions rather than rates.

Is the dataset reproducible end-to-end?

Yes. All randomness is seeded at the top of Stage 2 (random.seed(RANDOM_SEED), default 42). Stage 4 takes --seed explicitly. Stage 5 sorts procedure IDs before shuffling with np.random.default_rng(seed) so splits are machine-independent. Re-running with the same seed produces byte-identical output.

How are two same-class instruments (e.g. two graspers) disambiguated?

Every event tuple carries an inst_id alongside the class id. Stage 1 runs a geometric tracker (_assign_instance_ids_for_class) that propagates persistent instance ids via greedy nearest-centroid assignment, with a 1.0 s re-id window for brief occlusions. Generators key all tracks on (c_i, inst_id), and answer text gets an (instance 1) suffix only when two same-class instruments coexist — single-instance clips read identically to the pre-instance schema.

How does the train / val / test split prevent leakage?

A single procedure-to-split map is built once (Stage 5) and applied to both modalities. So a cholec_42 clip lands in the same split for video and image streams, and an assert_no_overlap check runs before any file is written. Procedure IDs are normalized via regex (cholec_\d+ / prostaTD_\w+) so different naming conventions collapse to the same key.

Where do the actual question texts live? Can I read them?

The question wording for every active subcategory is in QA_TEMPLATE_INVENTORY_AND_BRAINSTORM.md (Part I) with the answer schema, gate constants, and an assessment per template. The runtime templates are hardcoded inline in utils/data/create_surgSTU_qa_dataset.py; the standalone utils/data/qa_templates.py file mirrors them as documentation but is not used at runtime — editing it alone has no effect on generated data.

Limitations & roadmap

  • Workflow / phase-step queries are out of scope for this release because Stage 1 currently discards the phase and step columns.
  • Four templates retain top-1 share above 35 % on cholec after Phase 2.6 iteration 2 (most_active_instrument, idle_duration, comparison, rel_change). See FAQ #3.
  • Bbox provenance asymmetry: CholecT50 boxes come from CholecTrack20; ProstaTD boxes are internally annotated pending the official ProstaTD bbox release.
  • Refusal samples are capped at ~5 %. Tuning beyond that range risks over-refusal on legitimate queries.
  • Multi-instance disambiguation works end-to-end but has not yet been probed by an explicit "which-instance" template — planned for a follow-up release.

Acknowledgements & source datasets

SurgSTU stands entirely on publicly available surgical annotation work. The cholecystectomy half builds on CholecT50 (Nwoye et al.) for instrument-verb-target triplets and on CholecTrack20 for instrument bounding boxes. The prostatectomy half builds on ProstaTD for instrument-verb-target annotations; ProstaTD bounding boxes are internally annotated by the authors pending an official ProstaTD bbox release.

This work was conducted at the Institute of Medical Technology and Intelligent Systems (IMETUM), Hamburg University of Technology. We thank the maintainers of CholecT50, CholecTrack20, and ProstaTD for releasing their annotations openly.
