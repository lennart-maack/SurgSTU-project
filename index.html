SurgSTU

An Approach to Enriching Surgical Video Datasets for Fine-Grained Spatial-Temporal Understanding of Vision-Language Models

Lennart Maack*, 1 Alexander Schlaefer1
1 Institute of Medical Technology and Intelligent Systems, Hamburg University of Technology, Germany
*Corresponding author (lennart.maack@tuhh.de)
DataCV @ CVPR 2026 Workshop
Datasets Code Paper arXiv

Abstract

Surgical video understanding is a crucial prerequisite for advancing Computer-Assisted Surgery. While vision-language models (VLMs) have recently been applied to the surgical domain, existing surgical vision-language datasets lack in capturing and evaluating complex, interleaved spatial-temporal dynamics. Creating large scale datasets that accurately represent fine-grained spatial-temporal relationships in surgical videos is challenging due to costly manual annotations or error-prone generation using large language models.

To address this gap, we introduce the SurgSTU-Pipeline, a deterministic generation pipeline featuring temporal and spatial continuity filtering to reliably create surgical datasets for fine-grained spatial-temporal multimodal understanding. Applying this pipeline to publicly available surgical datasets, we create the SurgSTU dataset, comprising 6711 video clips densely extended with 150k fine-grained spatial-temporal question-answer samples.

Our comprehensive evaluation shows that while state-of-the-art generalist VLMs struggle in zero-shot settings, their spatial-temporal capabilities can be improved through in-context learning. A fine-tuned VLM on the SurgSTU training dataset achieves highest performance among all spatial-temporal tasks, validating the dataset's efficacy to improve spatial-temporal understanding of VLMs in surgical videos.

Question types

Three deterministic generators emit 36 active subcategories. Every question is filled from a Python template against a continuity- and interestingness-filtered window of Event Tuples — no LLM rewrites or paraphrases anywhere in the pipeline.

FAQ

How did you ensure the dataset isn’t dominated by “grasper grasps gallbladder” samples?

The pipeline applies dataset-level rejection sampling on 14 categorical templates using per-procedure, per-correct-answer accept maps. The orchestrator probabilistically drops samples whose correct answer is over-represented for the procedure. Concrete impact — the modal answer share on reverse_target dropped from 91 % to 51 %, on idle_duration from 93 % to 49 %, and similar reductions elsewhere.

A handful of templates still retain a top-1 share above 35 % (comparison Yes at 52 %, idle_duration [50%+] at 45 %, rel_change maintained at 46 %, most_active_instrument grasper at 41 %); we surface these openly rather than over-prune the corpus.

How did you ensure that motion and displacement are large enough to be evaluable?

Every motion-sensitive template has an interestingness gate. Trajectory requires an x-range or y-range above MIN_TRAJECTORY_RANGE = 0.20 (normalized). Relative-change requires |Δdistance| ≥ 0.30. Relative-position requires |dx| and |dy| both above 0.18. Time-window templates need ≥ 2 s of motion.

Failed gates re-roll; samples that consistently fail are dropped silently. Phase 2.6 also tightened the spatial-continuity gate to 0.6 normalized-diagonal/sec and the temporal-gap gate to 0.7 s, so trajectories built from bbox jitter are filtered out before they reach any template.

No LLM in the loop — really?

Yes. Every question and answer is filled from a deterministic Python template against normalized event tuples. No model paraphrases the questions and no model judges the answers. Re-running with the default random seed (42) produces byte-identical output.

Where do the bounding boxes come from? Same provenance for both procedures?

No — and this matters. CholecT50 boxes come from CholecTrack20. ProstaTD boxes are internally annotated by the authors pending the official ProstaTD bbox release. Both are normalized to the same (x1, x2, y1, y2) order against each video’s true frame dimensions.

How does the train / val / test split avoid leakage?

A single procedure-to-split map is built once and applied to both video and image modalities, so a cholec_42 clip is in the same split everywhere. An assert_no_overlap check runs before any file is written.

Are there refusal samples? Why?

Yes — about 5 % of the corpus. Two refusal templates: refusal_absent_instrument (asked about an instrument that never appears in the clip) and refusal_no_specific_action (asked about a verb when the instrument is idling). Without them a model learns to always produce a confident bbox or verb. Higher fractions push models toward over-refusal on legitimate queries.

Acknowledgements & source datasets

SurgSTU stands entirely on publicly available surgical annotation work. The cholecystectomy half builds on CholecT50 (Nwoye et al.) for instrument-verb-target triplets and on CholecTrack20 for instrument bounding boxes. The prostatectomy half builds on ProstaTD for instrument-verb-target annotations; ProstaTD bounding boxes are internally annotated by the authors pending an official ProstaTD bbox release.

This work was conducted at the Institute of Medical Technology and Intelligent Systems (IMETUM), Hamburg University of Technology. We thank the maintainers of CholecT50, CholecTrack20, and ProstaTD for releasing their annotations openly.
